## Case Brief (output of `prompt-template.md` run against the judgment above)

- **Citation:** M/S. Jindal (India) Ltd vs. The State of West Bengal & Ors, Calcutta High Court (Appellate Side), 2025:CHC-AS:1160, decided 1 July 2025 (Shampa Dutt (Paul), J.)

- **Procedural posture:** Challenge before the Calcutta High Court to an award of the 2nd Industrial Tribunal, Kolkata (dated 13 December 2024) arising from a termination/back-wages dispute.

- **Facts:** The employer-petitioner (Jindal (India) Ltd) contested an Industrial Tribunal award that dismissed the employee's claim for back wages. The Tribunal had disregarded cross-examination evidence going to the employee's post-termination employment, on the ground that this factual matter was not expressly pleaded in the employer's written statement.

- **Issue(s):** Whether evidence elicited in cross-examination can be disregarded solely because the underlying fact was not explicitly pleaded in the written statement, where the parties otherwise understood and litigated the substance of that issue.

- **Holding:** No. The Tribunal erred in disregarding the cross-examination evidence on a purely technical pleading ground; the award was set aside and the matter remanded for rehearing, with direction to consider all evidence including the previously excluded cross-examination.

- **Ratio decidendi:** Substance prevails over form in pleading-based exclusion of evidence — where the parties understood the essential issues in dispute and proceeded to trial on that basis, a technical pleading deficiency does not bar the tribunal from considering evidence (including cross-examination) that speaks to those issues. Cross-examination is an integral, not peripheral, part of testimony and cannot be discounted merely for a pleading gap.

- **Relief granted/denied:** Award of the Industrial Tribunal set aside; matter remanded for fresh hearing considering all evidence.

- **Key precedents cited:** *Mohammed Abdul Wahid* (Supreme Court) — cited for the principle that cross-examination is an important part of witness testimony and courts must look at substance over form in pleading disputes.

- **Practical note for counsel:** Directly useful where an opposing tribunal/court seeks to exclude cross-examination evidence on a pleading-technicality basis — this judgment (and the *Mohammed Abdul Wahid* line it relies on) supports arguing that the real test is whether the parties understood and litigated the issue, not whether it was pleaded with precision. Given this is a July 2025 Appellate Side decision, it's recent enough to be directly citable in current West Bengal industrial/labour disputes; verify it hasn't been challenged further before relying on it.
